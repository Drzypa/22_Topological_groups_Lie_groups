invariant scalar product


Let 𝕂 be a field and V a vector space over 𝕂. Let G be a group with a specified representation on V denoted by g.v for v∈V and g∈G.

An invariant scalar product (with respect to the action of G) on V is a scalar product (⋅|⋅) on V (i.e. a non-degenerate, symmetric 𝕂-bilinear form) such that for any g∈G,u,v∈V we have

(g.u|g.v)=(u|v)

Now let 𝔤 be a Lie algebra over 𝕂 with a representation on V denoted by X.v for X∈𝔤,v∈V. Then an invariant scalar product (with respect to the action of 𝔤) is a scalar product on V such that for any X∈𝔤,u,v∈V we have

(X.u|v)=-(u|X.v)

An invariant scalar product on a Lie algebra 𝔤 is by definition an invariant scalar product as above where the representation is the adjoint representation of 𝔤 on itself. In this case invariance is usualy written ([X,Y]∣Z)=(X∣[Y,Z])

1 Examples

For example if G=O⁢(n) the orthogonal subgroup of n×n real matricies and ℝn is the natural representation for O⁢(n), then the standard Euclidean scalar product on ℝn is an invariant scalar product. Invariance in this example follows from the definition of O⁢(n).

As another example if 𝔤 is a complex semi-simple Lie algebra then the Killing form κ⁢(X,Y):=T⁢r⁢(a⁢dX⋅a⁢dY) is an invariant scalar product on 𝔤 itself via the adjoint representation. Invariance in this example follows from the fact that the trace operator is associative, i.e. T⁢r⁢([Y,X]⋅Z)=-T⁢r⁢([X,Y]⋅Z)=-T⁢r⁢(X⋅[Y,Z]). Thus an invariant scalar product (with respect to a Lie algebra representation) is sometimes called an associative scalar product.
